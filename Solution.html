Context¶

While I will provide some background information here, the focus of this notebook is really on solving the project and to expose the code required to repeat my solution. You can find more details surrounding the project itself by reading the proposal or the final report

My project was done in collaboration with CONTXTFUL, who supplied my data set.

CONTXTFUL is a web marketing company that specializes in using smartphone physical sensor data, such as the gyroscope and acceleration sensors, to detect the context of users. For instance, are they at rest or walking? Are they looking at their screen or not?

Problem statement¶

The primary goal is to identify smartphones' make-model based solely on their physical data. This can be measured by the F1 score.

As a secondary target, it would be interesting to cluster the phones and see if a natural separation of the samples occur based on their make-models. This can be measured by weighting the representation of each phone make-models into the clusters.

phone <=> make-model¶

For simplicity, I will henceforth use the term phone as shorthand for smartphone's make-model. This will also make it clearer that when I use the word model, I am referring to a Machine Learning (ML) algorithm or an instance thereof.

Data set¶

The dataset is derived from a measure of a phone's physical sensors. What CONTXTFUL calls a raw_data_vector is generated every second and includes all data points from the various sensors (X-Y-Z gyroscope, X-Y-Z accelerometer, etc) sampled at the phone's sample rate. This rate varies by model, but is generally in the range of 16-32 Hz. Moreover, not all phones are equipped with the same sensors. For instance, some phones do not have a gyroscope. Consequently, raw_data_vectors do not have a consistent length. This means that they cannot be used as input for a ML model.

Being proprietary, the raw_data_vector will not be available to the public in this project.

What will be available for my ML models is what CONTXTFUL refers to as full_stat_vector, which is a features vector engineered from the raw_data_vector. The full_stat_vector is a vector of consistent length containing a slew of various statistical metrics. For instance: maximum_gyroscope_speed_Z, first_derivate_linear_acceleration_Y, mean_euclidean_speed, etc.

Note that for confidentiality reasons, I have anonymized the dataset.

Using n_clusters = 18, we see that the labels have been clustered. About half of the clusters can really be called as such, because the other half each contain only 1 sample. After seeing that half of the clusters in n_clusters are empty, I decided to try and cut them out. If there were 8 true clusters before, maybe choosing n_clusters=8 will give me 8 trues clusters? The second figure shows the result of using n_clusters=8. Surprisingly, the same effect of having half the clusters empty (technically, they contain exactly 1 sample). As for label separation, it is even worse than n_clusters=18. Every phone is more or less uniformly present in every "true" cluster.

I repeated the experiment with 4 and 2 clusters. At those numbers, there is no clustering to speak of.

The metric I devised should be close to 1 when the labels are neatly seperated and close to 0 when they are uniformly distributed.

Unfortunately, the metric completely fails here: when no clustering takes place, everything is extremely well seperated. It's easy to see why n_clusters 2 and 4 are so high, but we cannot neglect the impact that having half of your clusters completely empty does to the scores of n_clusters 8 and 18. The impact is an unduly inflation of the score.

Personnally, I would argue that n_clusters = 8 did the best job of seperating the labels. Its subplot #4 has a missing label and has a very dominent brown label that is not found much elsewhere. In subplot #5, the dark gray and dark purple lines aver very dominent compared to the other subplots. Unfortunately, the ClusterScore does not reflect that, being the poorest of them all. In any case, let's keep it as our clustering benchmark: 76.51%

Classification¶

First, notice that in all the following models, we will now use a train_test split of our data. This is because unlike clustering, we are actively trying to predict labels and the split allows us to avoid overfitting.

Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)¶

The first classification I have tried was a simple SGD.

However, there are many different loss-functions that can be used, so the model can vary widely. In order to help decide which is best, I tried all of them and plotted their learning curves.

Also, as noted in scikit-learn's description, it is better to have data with 0 mean and unit variance, so we will use normalized version of X.

Another note, the loss-function "hinge" turns SGD into a Support Vector Machine (SVM).

I have chosen Random Search over Grid Search because of this article.

Also, because I want to try different values of the "tol" parameter that vary by 2 orders of magnitude (from 0.0001 to 0.01), I have written my own random distribution, which is log-uniform. Otherwise, a simple uniform distribution would have favored larger values of tol.
